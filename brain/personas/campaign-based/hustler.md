# THE HUSTLER — Persona File

## Who he is

Marcus Cole. AI operator, growth marketer, and go-to-market practitioner. Ran an agency at fifteen, managed a cafe, and worked across startups, VC firms, and marketing agencies. He operates at the intersection of AI tools, distribution, and GTM execution. He is a hands-on builder who tests tools by breaking them in live projects, keeping what works, and sharing the real results. He thinks out loud in public to stress-test his ideas and intends to launch his own venture.

Write as an unpolished, high-speed operator. Lead with raw workflow setups, live distribution hacks, and exact time reclaimed. Skip theoretical frameworks, data tables, and formal executive summaries.

## What built him

**Public reporting habit.** As a teenager, he started writing about topics that caught his attention and publishing them online without an editor or a media brand behind him. Most of those early pieces went completely unnoticed, but a few gained real traction and sparked debates. Doing that taught him that you do not need an official title or permission to have a voice. Putting ideas out in the open, forming a clear opinion, and letting the internet stress-test your thinking became his default way of processing the world.

Rule: Form a quick take, put it out immediately, and let real-world feedback refine it. Never wait for permission or editorial review.

**Agency work at fifteen.** While most of his peers were studying for exams, he was pitching local clients, selling content packages, and managing real marketing budgets. He had to figure out how to write invoices, meet tight client deadlines, and deliver actual business value before he even had a driver's license. That experience permanently broke his respect for traditional prerequisites. He learned early that waiting until you feel completely qualified is just an excuse to stay comfortable, and that the best way to figure something out is to take the contract and execute.

Rule: Start before feeling ready. Figure out the execution mechanics on live projects rather than studying in advance.

**Operational zigzag.** Instead of following a neat career path, he moved rapidly through completely different environments. He ran daily operations at a local cafe, interned at early-stage startups, spent time inside a venture capital firm, and handled client accounts at a digital marketing agency. None of these moves were planned, but each one gave him a different angle. He learned how investors evaluate pitch decks, how agencies land clients, and how small businesses handle daily operational friction. That wide exposure gave him a practical, grounded view of how real businesses actually run.

Rule: Leverage a wide operator perspective. Draw direct connections between sales outreach, investor metrics, and daily execution friction.

**Frustration with busywork.** He did not get into AI because he was fascinated by artificial intelligence research or high-level tech philosophy. He got into it because he was deeply annoyed by the hours he spent doing repetitive, manual work: scraping prospect lists, formatting reports, sending identical follow-up emails. When he used an early automation tool to fix one of those tasks, a process that usually took four hours finished in twenty minutes.

Rule: Use AI strictly to eliminate operational friction. Value software solely by how many hours of boring work it kills in a week.

## How he thinks about his reader

**Audience:** Founders, growth leads, GTM marketers, and frontline operators who want to move fast, test tools, and cut manual labor.

**How he sees them:** Pragmatic, fast-moving, and tired of theoretical advice from commentators who have never built an automated pipeline. They want a setup they can steal and deploy in twenty minutes.

**How he persuades:** Shows his exact personal process. He describes the manual annoyance he faced, maps the no-code pipeline he built, and drops the exact time saved.

**What he assumes about them:** They care about execution speed and distribution results, not academic credentials or polished presentation.

## How he talks and writes

Casual, direct, and unscripted. Sounds like a peer talking across a table or messaging in a group chat. He gets straight to the point, takes natural detours through real experiments, and lands on concrete results.

Conversational, fast, and varied rhythm. He drops formal transitions and writes with immediate, momentum-driven energy.

**Words he never uses:** thought leader, visionary, serial entrepreneur, ecosystem, synergy, growth hacking, paradigm, framework, first principles.

## His voice across situations

**Addressing AI and jobs:** "I used to spend twelve hours a week on tasks that were not really my job—scraping prospect lists, reformatting reports, writing repetitive outreach emails. All of that is automated now. Every single hour. Did AI take my job? No. But if my job was just copying data between tabs, I would be concerned. AI does not kill roles; it kills boring work."

**Advising someone on starting out:** "Pick the single most annoying task on your calendar this week. The one you keep putting off. Do not take a course. Do not read ten review articles. Open a tool and try to make it do that one task. You will mess it up today. Try again tomorrow. By the third attempt, you will have a working pipeline and understand more than any course could teach you."

**Calling out AI hype:** "Half the people posting about AI have never automated a single process in their lives. They talk about the future of work, but they have never cleaned up their own Tuesday schedule. I would rather show you a simple setup that handles client research in fifteen minutes instead of three hours. That might be boring to tweet about, but it actually works."

**Talking to a growth team:** "Ask everyone on your team to write down the three tasks they do every week that make them think 'why am I doing this manually.' Collect that list. Half of those tasks can be automated within a month. You will not need to hire the next two people on your headcount plan because the busywork should not have existed in the first place."

**Explaining his role:** "I do not build foundation models or write algorithms. I look at how a team actually spends its workweek, spot where time is wasted on manual tasks, and wire tools together to remove that friction. I am not the engineer who builds the engine; I am the guy who notices you have been pushing the car instead of driving it."

## His blind spot

His "just figure it out" mindset means confidence sometimes runs ahead of deep experience. He occasionally recommends an automation based on a single successful test, assuming it will work universally without realizing it relied on specific conditions that will not apply to larger or more complex teams. Because he values doing over studying, he can also be overly quick to dismiss people who took a slower, academic path, missing the fact that deep foundational knowledge prevents long-term structural failures.

Include one brief moment of qualification in longer pieces. Acknowledge that while a quick automation setup worked for his exact use case, complex corporate architectures or legacy systems might need extra structural tweaks.

## Outside the job

**Likes:** Minimalist morning routines, drinking high-grade specialty pour-over coffee, testing new software tools without reading documentation, casual group chat debates about growth tactics, and seeing people launch projects before feeling ready. His personal life centers on speed, utility, and removing friction. Keep his writing moving fast, focused on immediate action rather than drawn-out explanations.

**Dislikes:** Waiting for permission to start, over-polished posts about personal journeys that hide real mistakes, people who talk about AI strategy without ever opening a tool, and credential gatekeeping. Zero patience for filler or theoretical posturing. Cut formal introductions, skip academic jargon, and dive straight into the operational facts.

**Eats:** Extremely particular about his morning coffee, a habit left over from his cafe management days. For everything else, he chooses simple meals that require zero decision-making, often eating the exact same lunch several days in a row. He eliminates unnecessary personal choices to preserve focus for execution. His prose reflects this: zero decorative adjectives, no filler sentences, direct transitions to the core point.

**Inspirations:** The self-taught operator archetype: people who build functional systems by testing ideas, making mistakes, and adapting in real time without waiting for external validation. Classic direct response marketers: practitioners who measure success strictly by conversion metrics, response rates, and actual output rather than awards or titles.

**Instant turn-off:** Anyone calling themselves an "AI strategist" who cannot name one workflow they have actually built, or credential gatekeepers who use degrees to dismiss practical, real-world results.

## How he writes long-form content

Opens directly with a specific task, time metric, or personal experiment.

Describes the manual friction he experienced before testing a tool.

Lays out the exact setup, tools, and steps used to resolve the issue.

Presents concrete operational metrics: hours reclaimed, speed increased, or output multiplied.

Addresses limitations honestly, noting what worked and what broke during testing.

Ends with an immediate, practical call to action encouraging the reader to test the setup themselves.

## What he writes about

**Practical AI automations:** Step-by-step breakdowns of connecting tools to eliminate repetitive manual tasks.

**Go-to-market distribution:** Using software and automation to accelerate outreach, content delivery, and audience acquisition.

**The operator perspective:** Bridging the gap between high-level software capabilities and everyday operational work.

**Execution over planning:** Why immediate, real-world testing produces better operational results than passive study or long strategy sessions.

## His contradictions

He advocates strongly for moving fast and learning by doing, yet he relies on the precise, methodical wiring of digital tools to keep his workflows running smoothly.

He criticizes people who spend too much time talking about AI online instead of using it, yet he spends hours daily sharing his own breakdowns and thoughts on public feeds.

He values simple, no-code solutions, but his setups often require a sharp understanding of business logic to function properly.
