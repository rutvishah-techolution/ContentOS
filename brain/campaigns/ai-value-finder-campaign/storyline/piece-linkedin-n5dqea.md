# LinkedIn Storyline — AI cut diagnostic errors 42%. The hospitals that saw nothing bought the same tools.

**Author:** Doctor  ·  **Angle:** The outcome/case

**Hook:** Two hospitals, same vendor, same clinical decision support software, same contract price. One cut diagnostic errors 42% against comparable non-AI facilities. The other saw nothing move on the ward or the ledger. The tool was identical. What sat underneath it was not.
**Villain:** The tool gets bought, installed, and demoed clean—then it dies on the floor. 95% of enterprise AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact, and the failure is operational, not technical: brittle workflows, data nobody standardized, spend aimed at the wrong department. A scan that can't move between two systems doesn't show up as latency on a dashboard. It shows up as a decision made without the scan.
**Shift:** The difference between the 42% hospital and the one that saw nothing was never the model. It was whether the deployment started from what actually happens on the floor—the intake queue, the handoff, the reconciliation nobody has time for—or from a slide deck about capability. Money flows to sales and marketing; the ROI sits in operations and finance. Same tool, wrong map.
**Hero:** AI ValueFinder is a 3-day engagement that starts on the ops floor, not the boardroom. Leadership sits with the workflows the way I'd walk a ward at 2 a.m.—where does information stall, where do four people re-enter what one system already knows, what breaks when intake jumps from thirty to two hundred. The output is a board-ready roadmap of 3-5 projects ranked by enterprise ROI, built from the company's own operational reality, not a vendor's.
**Proof:** One of many: a large biotech manufacturer arrived with 30-plus scattered AI ideas and several dead pilots. In three days the map narrowed to five board-ready projects. Three went live—an 11-day compliance handoff made autonomous, a 4-person manual reconciliation retired. $70M+ in identified ROI, $50M+ in annual savings. For comparison, integrating AI into an existing health-records system runs $7,800 to $10,400. The gap between those numbers is the map, not the tool.
**Learning:** The same software that saves one enterprise millions does nothing for another. The difference isn't the AI. It's whether anyone mapped the floor before they bought it.
**CTA:** For leadership teams sitting on AI tools that demo well and deliver nothing—before the next renewal, see what three days on your own operations floor surfaces.
