# Blog Storyline — Rented Intelligence: The Vendor Lock-In Risk Hiding in Your AI Stack

**Author:** Policy Insider  ·  **Angle:** The cautionary pattern

**Hook:** A CFO reviewing next year's renewal notices finds a line item that wasn't there before: a 20-37% price hike labeled an 'AI tax,' applied by a legacy vendor regardless of whether the tools were adopted. Seventy-eight percent of IT leaders report the same unexpected charges. The question is not whether the stack is expensive. It is who now controls the terms.
**Villain:** Dependency on third-party AI models that hardens over time into vendor lock-in, concentration risk, and business-continuity exposure—what KPMG names 'rented intelligence.' The enterprise does not own the intelligence it runs on; it leases it, and the lessor sets the price and the data terms.
**Shift:** Most leadership treats AI procurement as a technology-acquisition decision: pick the best model, sign the contract. The structural reality is that the vendor relationship is a governance and continuity decision. When Microsoft—which writes up to 30% of its code with GenAI—internally restricted its own employees' access to Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 in June 2026 over data-retention terms while still selling that same model to customers, it demonstrated that even the largest buyers treat vendor terms as a live risk, not a settled purchase.
**Hero:** The insight: whether an AI capability strengthens or endangers the enterprise is determined not by model quality but by ownership and grounding. Externally-built tools succeed twice as often as internal builds (MIT), yet heavy external spend—up to $7,500 per employee monthly at top firms—produces no durable advantage. The differentiator is capabilities anchored to the company's own operational workflows and data, so the enterprise retains control of the thing that produces the value. Value that lives inside your workflows cannot be repriced on renewal; value that lives inside a vendor's model can.
**Proof:** The pattern shows on both sides. On the exposure side: enterprise AI bills rose ~320% even as per-token prices fell 98%, average corporate AI budgets climbed from $1.2M in 2024 to $7M in 2026, and Uber burned its full 2026 AI coding budget in four months—with its COO conceding token usage 'didn't seem to correlate directly with useful features shipped.' On the ownership side: where AI is anchored to specific operational workflows, 93.7% of Fortune 1000 companies report measurable business value, and Boston Children's Hospital with Harvard established 18 new diagnoses from 376 previously unsolved rare-disease cases—value grounded in their own clinical reality, not rented from a general-purpose stack.
**Learning:** You cannot be locked out of intelligence you built into your own operations. Rented intelligence can always be repriced; owned workflows cannot.
**CTA:** The hardest part is knowing which capabilities are worth grounding in your own operational reality and which are safe to rent—a mapping question we work on with leadership teams before the next renewal cycle sets the terms for them.
