# Blog — Draft 1

# 53% of Your Software Licenses Sit Unused. Your AI Budget Is Making the Same Bet Blind.

Real talk for a second.

Right now, roughly **53% of the software licenses your company pays for are barely touched or completely unused** — bleeding up to $80.6M a year at large enterprises, with IT controlling just 15% of that SaaS spend.

Now watch that exact same buying reflex get pointed at AI, where 59% of companies already spend over $1M a year on it.

You see where this is going.

## The habit you didn't know you had

Here's the villain, and it's not a vendor. It's a reflex.

Companies bought SaaS the way you'd stock a supply closet — acquire the capability, hope the usage shows up later. It rarely did. Now that same instinct has a new invoice with "AI" on it.

And the numbers are brutal. Of $30–40B in enterprise GenAI investment, about **95% of pilots produced zero measurable P&L impact** (MIT). PwC asked 4,454 CEOs across 95 countries how AI performed — 56% said it delivered *neither* cost nor revenue benefit.

That's not an AI problem. That's shelfware wearing a costume.

## The part nobody in the executive suite mentions

Here's what the 5% who actually made money did differently.

They didn't buy better models. They wired AI into *one specific, high-value process* instead of bolting more tools onto the org chart.

And — this is the part that surprised me — the payback wasn't in the flashy sales-and-marketing pilots everyone funds first. It was in the unglamorous back-office grind. Document review. Reconciliation. Customer support.

MIT tracked **$2–10M in annual savings** and **30% cuts in outside agency spend** in exactly those quiet corners.

The money was hiding in the work nobody wanted to demo.

## Start on the floor, not in the boardroom

This is where **AI ValueFinder** comes in — a 3-day engagement that starts on your operations floor, mapping where your people actually lose hours copying and pasting between systems.

At the end, leadership gets a board-ready roadmap: **3–5 projects ranked by real ROI**. So you're funding workflows with measurable payback, not licenses nobody opens.

One large biotech manufacturer walked in with 30+ scattered AI ideas and several failed pilots — the classic shelfware pattern, just with "AI" on the invoice.

Three days later, that pile was filtered to 5 projects tied to *their* operational reality: an 11-day compliance handoff made autonomous, a 4-person manual reconciliation replaced.

Three went live. Result: **$70M+ in identified ROI, $50M+ in annual savings.**

One engagement of many.

One honest caveat before you get excited like I did: whatever workflow you pick, run it through your IT security and compliance teams before you connect real enterprise data. Speed is great; a data mess is not.

## The line to remember

You don't have an AI budget problem. You have a shelfware habit wearing a new invoice.

If your team has bought plenty of AI and still can't point to the line on the P&L where it paid off, spend three days finding out which three workflows actually would — before your IT and compliance teams get handed another tool to vet.
