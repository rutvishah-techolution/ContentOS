# Blog — Final

# Rented Intelligence: The Vendor Lock-In Risk Hiding in Your AI Stack

A CFO reviewing next year's renewals finds a line item that wasn't there before: a 20-37% price hike labeled an "AI tax," applied by a legacy vendor whether or not the tools were ever adopted. Seventy-eight percent of IT leaders report the same unexpected charges. The question is not whether the stack is expensive. It is who now controls the terms.

The villain is not the price hike. It is the structure underneath it. Dependency on third-party models hardens over time into vendor lock-in, concentration risk, and business-continuity exposure. KPMG calls this "rented intelligence": the enterprise does not own the intelligence it runs on. It leases it, and the lessor sets both the price and the data terms. That distinction sounds semantic until the renewal notice arrives.

Most leadership teams treat AI procurement as a technology-acquisition decision. Pick the best model, sign the contract, move on. But the vendor relationship is a governance and continuity decision, and evaluating it as one is where the advantage sits. The clearest signal came from the largest possible buyer. Microsoft, which writes up to 30% of its own code with generative AI, internally restricted its employees' access to Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 in June 2026 over data-retention terms, while still selling that same model to its own customers. When the most sophisticated buyer in the market treats vendor terms as a live risk rather than a settled purchase, the terms are the product.

Here is what reframes the whole question. Whether an AI capability strengthens or endangers the enterprise is determined by ownership and grounding, not model quality. MIT's data shows externally-built tools succeed twice as often as internal builds, so the answer is not "build everything yourself." Yet heavy external spend, up to $7,500 per employee monthly at top firms, produces no durable advantage. The differentiator is capabilities anchored to the company's own operational workflows and data. Value that lives inside your workflows cannot be repriced on renewal. Value that lives inside a vendor's model can.

The pattern shows on both sides of the ledger. On the exposure side, enterprise AI bills rose roughly 320% even as per-token prices fell 98%, and average corporate AI budgets climbed from $1.2M in 2024 to $7M in 2026. Uber burned its full 2026 AI coding budget in four months. Its COO conceded that token usage "didn't seem to correlate directly with useful features shipped." That is what rented intelligence looks like when the meter runs faster than the value.

On the ownership side, the numbers invert. Where AI is anchored to specific operational workflows, 93.7% of Fortune 1000 companies report measurable business value. Boston Children's Hospital, working with Harvard, analyzed 376 previously unsolved rare-disease cases and, after expert review, established 18 new diagnoses. That value was grounded in their own clinical reality: their cases, their records, their workflows. Nothing rented from a general-purpose stack a vendor could reprice or restrict tomorrow.

The tension deserves its strongest counterargument before it deserves agreement. Not everything should be owned. Building your own models is where 95% of pilots fail, and the "rent versus own" line is not a moral one. Some capabilities are perfectly safe to lease. The discipline is knowing which is which before the contract, not after.

Rented intelligence can always be repriced. Workflows you built into your own operations cannot be locked away from you.

Which capabilities are worth grounding in your own operational reality, and which are safe to rent, is a mapping question. We work through it with leadership teams before the next renewal cycle sets the terms for them.
