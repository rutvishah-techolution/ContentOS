# Blog — Draft 1

# The Internal AI Build That Cost $7M and Shipped Nothing: Why Externally-Built Tools Succeed Twice as Often

A Fortune 500 division wrote 30% of its code with generative AI, ran through a $7M annual AI budget, and then stopped using its own internal coding assistant. Uber burned its entire 2026 AI coding budget in four months. Its COO conceded that token usage "didn't seem to correlate directly with useful features shipped to users." The spend was real. The P&L impact was zero.

That is the pattern, not the exception. Average corporate AI budgets climbed from $1.2M in 2024 to $7M in 2026. Enterprise AI bills rose roughly 320% even as per-token prices fell 98%—a GPT-4-level workload dropped from about $20 to about $0.40 per million tokens. The money is moving. The value is not.

## The build starts in the wrong room

Here is the line item most boards never see: MIT's data shows 95% of AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact, and the failure is operational, not technical. Not model quality. Brittle workflows, no contextual learning, and misalignment with how the business actually runs.

The internal build is where this concentrates. Teams launch it from the boardroom and the roadmap deck—the org chart's view of the company. But the ROI does not sit on the org chart. It sits in operations and finance, where money isn't flowing. The same MIT data confirms it: budgets pour into sales and marketing while the better returns wait in operations, and externally-built tools succeed twice as often as internal builds.

The assumption is that whoever knows the company best should build the tool in-house. The data inverts that. Internal teams are too close to the operation to audit it. They build what the roadmap describes, not what the floor does. External builds win not because the models are better—everyone rents the same models at the same 98%-cheaper price—but because they start from operational reality the internal team stopped seeing years ago.

## An external audit of where the money actually moves

This is the gap Techolution's AI ValueFinder is built to close. It is a 3-day engagement, and it begins on the operations floor, not in the boardroom. Leadership does not hand over a wishlist. The team maps the actual workflows—the handoffs, the manual reconciliations, the compliance queues—then returns a board-ready roadmap of 3 to 5 projects ranked by enterprise ROI.

It reads less like a vendor pitch and more like a forensic audit: line items, not slides. Where does money leave the building, and which workflow, made autonomous, brings it back.

One of many engagements: a large biotech manufacturer arrived with 30-plus scattered AI ideas and several failed internal pilots. ValueFinder filtered them to 5 board-ready projects in 3 days. Three deployed—an 11-day compliance handoff made autonomous, a 4-person manual reconciliation replaced. The result: $70M+ in identified ROI and $50M+ in annual savings.

The ideas that failed weren't the wrong technology. They were the wrong map.

That distinction is the whole game. An internal build audits the roadmap. An external one audits the operation.

Only one of them shows up on the P&L.

For leadership teams sitting on failed internal pilots and a roadmap built from the org chart rather than the ops floor—three days will show you which line items actually move.
